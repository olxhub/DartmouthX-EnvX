ENERGY UNITS

In the International System of Units (SI), the unit of work or energy is the Joule (J). 

Power is the rate at which energy is used. The unit of power is the Watt (W), named after James Watt, who perfected the steam engine:

                                                            1 Watt = 1 Joule/second

Power is sometimes measured in horsepower (hp):

                                                            1 horsepower = 746 Watts

Electrical energy is generally expressed in kilowatt-hours (kWh):

                                                            1 kilowatt-hour = 3,600,000 Joules.

It is important to realize that a kilowatt-hour is a quantity of energy not power. For example, an electric motor that uses 2,000 Watts and is used for one hour would consume 2,000 watts X 1 hr = 2,000 watt-hours of energy, which is 2 kilowatt-hours. Electricity bills are typically reported in kilowatt-hours.

Heat energy is often measured in calories. One calorie (cal) is defined as the heat required to raise the temperature of 1 gram water 1º C:

                                                           1 calorie = 4.189 Joules

A non-SI unit that is still used in the United States is the British Thermal Unit (BTU). It is defined as the energy required to raise the temperature of 1 pound of water 1º Fahrenheit.
