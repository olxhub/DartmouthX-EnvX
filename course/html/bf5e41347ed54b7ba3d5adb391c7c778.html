Conservation and Efficiency

Every energy source, even renewable ones like wind and solar, has environmental repercussions. As such, the best approach in working towards sustainability is to reduce energy use altogether through conservation and efficiency. Conservation involves using less energy—turning off your computer, for example, or walking to work instead of driving. Closely related to this is efficiency, which refers to obtaining the same amount of work for a smaller amount of energy, like using a more efficient LED light bulb instead of an incandescent. Improving energy efficiency and conservation can save energy and save money: according to the U.S. Department of Energy, in 2012 the average U.S. family spent close to $2,000 a year on energy, with 20% or more of that energy wasted on poor insulation, air leaks, and inefficient appliances.

A selection of consumer LED bulbs that can replace incandescent bulbs

In order to reduce greenhouse gas emissions significantly, we will have to adopt many different approaches, from increasing the use of renewable energy, to conserving and making energy usage more efficient. At this moment, energy conservation and efficiency are the cheapest, fastest, and most environmentally sound options for both reducing environmental impact and maximizing our energy resources. They are also easy measures to implement, as they require small changes to existing systems, rather than switching to new technology. Therefore, instead of buying that new solar panel or hybrid car, the first thing you should do when trying to reduce your environmental impact is to cut down on energy use through both conservation and efficiency measures. As Amory Lovins, the chief scientist at the Rocky Mountain Institute, and an expert on energy efficiency says, “The best energy choices are the ones you don’t need.”
