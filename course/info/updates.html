Dear ENVX Class Member,

The second half of ENVX is now available. We will explore the following questions:

“Non-Renewable Energy” (Week 4): 

  • How do we evaluate coal, oil and natural gas from an environmental science perspective?
  • What regulates energy conversions and losses in human-built systems? 
  • How much energy “flows through your fingertips?”

We take you on a tour of the Dartmouth Powerplant and have a conversation with Dartmouth Sustainability Director, Rosi Kerr. 

“Renewable Energy” (Week 5):

  • What is renewable energy? 
  • How do we evaluate hydro, solar, wind and biomass from an environmental science perspective?
  • How can we increase efficiency and conservation?

We take a field trip to a PV tracker solar system, visit a straw bale house and have a conversation about waste vegetable oil as an energy source in India.

“Global Change” (Week 6):

  • What is global change and how is it influenced by population numbers, and food and energy choices? 
  • What are some of the effects of global change on humans and natural ecosystems?
  • What are additional factors in evaluating personal environmental impact?

ENVX Topics:

Intro to Environmental Science: We will explore the field of environmental science and how to think like an environmental scientist. What is the difference between environmental science and environmental studies? How do both differ from environmentalism? Why is energy so important in environmental science? 

The Human Population: Understanding the human population and the ways in which changes in the population affect the environment is essential to environmental science. How many people are there living on Earth and where do they live? What is the carrying capacity of Earth?  

Food and Soils: Part of understanding human carrying capacity depends on knowledge of agriculture, soils and the environmental implications of eating different types of food. How does eating meat or vegetables, local or organic, sustainable or industrial foods affect the environment?

Non-Renewable Energy: Almost every aspect of our lives is dependent on energy. Non-renewable fossil fuels are an important source of energy in much of the world. What are the advantages and disadvantages to using coal, petroleum and natural gas? How does each contribute to greenhouse gas emissions? 

Renewable Energy: The use of renewable fuels such as wind and solar is growing rapidly around the world.  What are the benefits and consequences  of using each? Are “green” energy sources the answer to the world’s energy needs? 

Global Change: Human impact on biodiversity and global change are arguably the integrating units of environmental science. How are we affecting global change and biodiversity? How can we reconcile human population growth, resource demands and sustainability?
