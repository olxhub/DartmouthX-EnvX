Intro to Environmental Science (Week 1)

Video: Definitions and Terms

Environmental Science: examines interactions among natural systems and human systems

System: any set of interacting components that influence one another by exchanging energy or materials 

Environmental Studies: a broader field that includes environmental science as well as policy, economics, and ethics

Environmentalism: a social movement that seeks to protect the environment through lobbying, activism, and education

Sustainability: living on Earth and using resources in a way that will allow future generations to do so with roughly the same standard of living 

Ecosystem services: functions provided by the natural environment that benefit humans

Video: Rock Snot

Invasive species: introduced species that expand rapidly when released

Introduced (alien) species: species living outside of their typical geographic range

Video: Matter and Energy 

Matter: anything that occupies space and contains mass

Mass: the amount of matter an object contains

Weight: the force that results from the action of gravity on mass

Atom: smallest particle that can contain the chemical properties of an element

Element: substance composed of atoms that cannot be broken down into smaller, simpler components

Molecules: particles that contain more than one atom

Compounds: molecules that contain more than one element

Composition of Atoms: Protons (+ charge), Electrons (- charge), and Neutrons (no charge) 

System: any set of interacting components that influence one another by exchanging energy or materials 

Chemical Reaction: when atoms separate from the molecules they are part of or combine with other molecules=

Law of Conservation of Matter: matter cannot be created nor destroyed; it can only change form

Energy: the ability to do work or transfer heat

Joule: a unit of energy; one joule is needed to light a one watt lightbulb for one second 

Calorie: a unit of energy; the amount of energy needed to raise one gram of water one degree centigrade

First Law of Thermodynamics: energy can neither be created nor destroyed, only transformed from one form to another

Second Law of Thermodynamics: when energy is transformed there is always a loss in energy quality (the ability to do work decreases) 

Video: Biodiversity

Biodiversity: a measure of the diversity of lifeforms on Earth

Scales of Biodiversity: species, ecosystem, and genetic

Species Diversity: the number of species in a region or particular habitat

Species: a group of organisms distinct from other groups in morphology (body form and structure), genetics, behavior, or biochemical properties

Ecosystem Diversity: a measure of the diversity of ecosystems or habitats in a given region

Genetic Diversity: a measure of the genetic variation among individuals in a population

Video: Systems and Feedbacks

System: any set of interacting components that influence one another by exchanging energy or materials 

Open System: exchanges of matter or energy occur across system boundaries

Closed System: matter or energy is not exchanged across system boundaries

System Analysis: the study of the inputs, outputs, and changes in a system under various conditions 

Steady State: when a system’s inputs and outputs are equal (net flux = 0) 

Mean Residence Time (MRT): average time a typical molecule remains in the system

MRT = (Volume of pool) / Flux 

Feedback: adjustments in input or output rates caused by changes to a system

Positive Feedback: amplifies change in a system by causing further increase or decrease 

Negative Feedback: resists change by returning to original state or slowing rate of change

Video: Hydrologic Cycle 

Hydrologic Cycle: movement of water above, through, and below the biosphere

Evapotranspiration: the process by which the sun moves moisture from the earth and plants to the atmosphere

Hydrologic Equation: precipitation = evapotranspiration + storage + runoff

Video: The Carbon Cycle

Photosynthesis: in plants, energy from the sun converts CO2 and H2O into sugar and oxygen 

Respiration: biomass is converted back into CO2

Sedimentation: carbon is deposited at the bottom of ocean and freshwater systems

Extraction: fossilized carbon (fossil fuel is extracted for energy use by humans) 

Abiotic: a process that does not involve biology or living organisms 

The Human Population (Week 2) 

Video: The World Today 

Growth Rate: rate at which a population changes over a year

Carrying Capacity: number of organisms a given habitat can hold indefinitely

Malthusian View: human population could one day exceed Earth’s carrying capacity

Video: Population Numbers

Growth Rate: [(births + in-migration) - (deaths + out-migration)] / total population

Doubling Time: an estimate of the time it takes for a population to double

The rule of 70: Doubling time = 70 / % growth rate

Crude Birth Rate (CBR): number of liver births per 1000 individuals in a population per year

Crude Death Rate (CDR): number of deaths per 1000 individuals in a population per year

Global population growth rate: (CBR - CDR) / 10

Total Fertility Rate (TFR): estimate of average number of children that each woman in a population will bear throughout her childbearing years

Replacement Level Fertility: total fertility rate required to offset the average number of deaths in the population so the current population size remains stable

Infant Mortality: number of deaths before the age of one year per 1000 live births in the population 

Immigration: people moving into a country

Emigration: people moving out of a country 

Net Migration Rate: difference between immigration and emigration in a given year per 1000 people in a country 

Positive Net Migration: more immigration than emigration

Negative Net Migration: more emigration than immigration

Life Expectancy: average number of years that an infant born in a particular year in a particular country can be expected to live

Video: How and Why Populations Change

Population Age Structure: how individuals are distributed across age ranges

Age Structure Diagrams: visual representations of age structure within a country, usually divided by male and female 

Theory of Demographic Transition: as a country moves from a subsistence economy to industrialization and increased affluence, it undergoes a predictable shift in population growth 

Video: Global Population Dynamics

IPAT Equation: Impact = Population x Affluence x Technology 

Food and Soils (Week 3) 

Video: Agriculture and Human Nutrition 

Organic Agriculture: production of crops without the use of synthetic pesticides or fertilizers 

Malnutrition: result of a diet that lacks the proper balance of carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins and minerals

Undernutrition: not consuming enough calories to be healthy 

Food Insecurity: condition in which people do not have adequate access to food

Famine: when food insecurity is so extreme that large numbers of deaths occur in a given area over a relatively short people of time

Overnutrition: ingestion of too many calories and improper foods that cause a person to become overweight 

Video: Modern Agriculture Effects (Part 1) 

Succession: the natural process of change and development in an ecosystem over time

Energy Subsidy: amount of energy required for each calorie of food produced 

Monoculture: growing one crop species in a given area

Video: Modern Agriculture Effects (Part 2)

Primary nutrients in fertilizer: nitrogen (N), phosphorous (P), potassium (K)

Organic Fertilizer: composed of organic matter from plants and animals, such as compost and manure

Synthetic Nitrogen Fertilizer: created by combusting natural gas to fix (capture nitrogen gas from the atmosphere into solid form

Green Revolution: significantly increased crop outputs by intensified use of fertilizer and water, and expanded plant breeding

Insecticides: target insect pests

Herbicides: target plant species that compete with crops 

Video: Modern Agriculture Alternatives

Organic Agriculture: aspires to work with natural systems rather than dominate them

Goals of Organic Agriculture: enhance biodiversity, keep organic matter and nutrients in soil, maintain soil, avoid synthetic fertilizers and synthetic pesticides 

Sustainable Agriculture: enhancing the quality of the soil, minimizes use of resources, and promotes economic viability 

Intercropping: when two or more crop species are planted together to promote synergistic interaction 

Agroforestry: intercropping trees with vegetables or other crops 

Contour Plowing: plowing and harvesting parallel to the topographic contours of the land

No-till Agriculture: leaving crop residue in fields between growing seasons 

Genetically Modified: a gene with a desirable trait from one organism is inserted into another organism 

Maximum Sustainable Yield: maximum amount of a renewable resource that can be harvested without compromising its future viability 

Video: The Soil Resource 

Soil Forming Factors: parent material, climate, topography, organisms, time

Parent Material: rock from which inorganic components of soil are derived 

Climate: long-term accumulation of weather events in a given environment

Topography: surface slope and arrangement of a landscape 

Organisms: the plants, animals and microorganisms in a soil 

Time: soils develop and mature as they age

Horizon: a layer of soil material with different characteristics than layers above and below 

O Horizon: layer of organic material in various stages of decomposition 

A Horizon: layer of mixed organic and mineral material

E Horizon: layer of leaching and eluviation found above the B horizon; only in forests 

B Horizon: also know as subsoil, mainly composed of minerals with little organic material

C Horizon: also known as subsoil, primarily unaltered parent material 

Porosity: how quickly a soil drains 

Loam: a soil composed of approximately 40% silt, 40% sand, and 20% clay

Cation-exchange Capacity (CEC): ability of a soil to absorb and release positively charged ions (cations) 

Base Saturation: the proportion of soil bases to soil acids

Soil Bases: calcium, magnesium, potassium and sodium 

Soil Acids: aluminum and hydrogen (detrimental for plant growth) 

Base Saturation: (soil bases) / (soil bases + soil acids) 

Soil Degradation: loss of ability to support plant growth

Soil Erosion: removal of top layers of soil by water or wind

Video: A Soil Profile

Spodosol: a soil characterized by an organic (O) horizon underlain by a leached (E) horizon

C.S.A.: community supported agriculture

Video: Energy Concepts

Energy: the ability to do work or transfer heat

Energy Units: joules (J), calorie (c), British Thermal Unit (BTU), kilowatt-hour (kWh)

Joule: one joule is needed to light a one watt lightbulb for one second

Power = Energy / Time

Units of Power: watts, kilowatts

Potential Energy: energy stored but not yet released

Chemical Energy: potential energy stored in chemical bonds of a material

Efficiency = (work in the form you want) / (total work of energy input to the system)  

Quality: the ease with which an energy source can be converted from one form to another

Entropy: disorder or randomness of a system

1st Law of Thermodynamics: energy can neither be created nor destroyed, only transformed from one form to another

2nd Law of Thermodynamics: when energy is transformed, its quantity remains the same but ability to do work diminishes

Video: Coal, Oil and Natural Gas

Emissions From Burning Coal: sulfur dioxide (SO2), carbon dioxide (CO2), particulates, trace metals

Disadvantages of Coal as Fuel: mining harms people and environment, large amounts of emissions from burning coal

Petroleum: fossil fuel hydrocarbon refined to produce gasoline, diesel fuels and other oils

Advantages of Natural Gas: burns efficiently, releases 50% less CO2 than coal

Global Warming Potential (GWP): CO2 = 1, CH4 = 25

Disadvantages of Natural Gas: extraction can be harmful to the environment, requires pipeline, methane leaks contribute to global warming

Renewable Energy: energy form that is non-depletable or potentially renewable

Global Change (Week 6)

Video: Global Change Basics

Global Change: change that occurs in the chemical, biological and physical properties of Earth

Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC): an international group of scientists working under the United Nations to research climate change

Weather: short term description of temperature, winds and rainfall

Climate: average weather that occurs in a given region over a long period of time

Global Warming: warming of oceans, landmasses and atmosphere of Earth

Radiative Forcing: quantifies the energy flux to and from the earth (Radiative Forcing on Earth is positive)

Negative radiative forcing: Earth gets cooler

Video: Greenhouse Gases

Global Warming Potential (GWP): amount of infrared energy that a given gas will absorb or retain

Carbon Dioxide (CO2): main anthropogenic sources are combustion of fossil fuels and reduction in vegetation

Methane (CH4): main anthropogenic sources are anaerobic (without oxygen) decomposition (GWP=25)

Nitrous Oxide (N2O): main anthropogenic sources are nitrate-rich soils and other flooded areas of land (GWP=125-300)

Chloroflourocarbons (CFCs): main anthropogenic source is creation for use in refrigerators and air conditioners (GWP=1,500-10,000)

Water (H2O): main anthropogenic source is increased evapotranspiration

Current Atmospheric CO2 Concentration: 400 part per million (ppm)

Video: Global Change Effects

Negative Feedback Loops: return a system to its original state

Positive Feedback Loops: move system away from equilibrium

Video: Synthesizing Global Change and Environmental Science

Ocean Acidification: leads to destruction of coral reefs by dissolving calcium carbonate

Thermal Expansion: an increase in water temperature leads to an increase in volume, causing sea level to rise as oceans warm

Pied Flycatcher: bird that synchronizes egg hatching with the hatching of its primary food source, caterpillars
